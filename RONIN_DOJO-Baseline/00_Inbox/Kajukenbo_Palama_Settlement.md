CORRECTING AN HISTORICAL MISCONCEPTION ABOUT KAJUKENBO

So much that is written about Kajukenbo claims the art was created at Palama Settlement. Based on what you know, is that your belief? It seems to be a common misconception. 

Let me start by saying research shows around April of 1947, Frank Ordonez invited Adriano Emperado to train with him, Joseph Holck, and Peter Choo at Choo's family home, 574 P Road in Damon Tract. George "Clarence" Chang was also present. Ordonez has credited Choo with inviting Chang, but in a recorded interview featuring Emperado, Holck, and Choo, all three agreed that Ordonez actually invited Chang. Nonetheless, all five of the founders began training together on that day at Choo's family home. 

Between April of 1947, and the later part of 1949, the founders combined their knowledge of karate, judo, jujitsu, kenpo, and boxing (Western and Chinese styles) to create a series of self-defense techniques that could be used in the street against a trained fighter grabbing them or throwing a boxer style punch at their head. The techniques also included self-defense applications against a criminal attacking with a knife or club. 

During interviews Emperado, Holck, and Choo explained that their training locations included Choo's home in Damon Tract, Kaheka gym, and an abandoned military barracks at Halawa Veteran's Housing, across the road from where Ordonez lived. 

In a 1987 interview conducted by Vince Black and featuring Emperado and Holck, the conversation about where they trained came up and Holck stated that the founders spent two years of their time together training in the military barracks across from Ordonez's home. That would have amounted to the vast majority of their time together. 

Research shows the founders concluded their combination system sometime between August and October of 1949. Once the system was finished Holck came up with the name KA-JU-KEN-BO by combining letters from their various arts. 

To conclude, the founders finished their combination system in 1949 and then Holck gave the art a name. The group then disbanded when Holck left for infantry training in Georgia. At no time did the founders ever train together at Palama Settlement. 

Now, based on what Holck shared about how much time the founders did spend together working on their art at the military barracks across from where Ordonez live, I believe if we are trying to lock down a location for the creation of Kajukenbo, my money would be on the abandoned military barracks at Halawa Veteran's Housing. 

It appears the misconception about the origins of Kajukenbo comes from the fact that around 1950-1952 (Research documents conflict with oral history) Emperado began teaching kenpo jiu-jitsu at Palama Settlement. Then in the fall of 1957, he changed the name of his art from kenpo jiu-jitsu to Kajukenbo and adopted the name Kajukenbo Self-Defense Institute as the name of his organization. 

While no one knows why Emperado failed to use the Kajukenbo name until 1957, the reality is the name was created by the founders 8 years earlier--and before Emperado began teaching at Palama Settlement. 

Emperado definitely added to the Kajukenbo curriculum while at Palama Settlement, but to be technically correct the art was created by the founders between 1947 and 1949 and named in 1949--so prior to Emperado moving to the Palama Settlement.
