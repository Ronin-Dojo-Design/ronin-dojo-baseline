# Research Review — MMB Services & Pricing

Overnight /rr lane (SESSION_0645, wave 2). Purpose: ground the RDD → Mammoth Metal Buildings
(Michael Flores) services pitch in US small-business market data, evaluate
commission-on-lead-conversion pricing, study the construction/metal-building niche (including the
operator-pointed Structure Webworks comp and the "PMBA" association question), and recommend the
pricing-model matrix RDD presents to Michael.

**Operator-pinned constraints (anchor; research contextualizes, never overrides):**

- Site build one-time **$8K–$10K** (supersedes the earlier $5–10K number); sanity math ≈ 80 hrs at
  $100–200/hr. Hourly band: **$100–200/hr**.
- **Firm-fixed-price only with tight pre-defined scope + change control** — any change, even a font
  color, is a written change order. Otherwise time & materials.
- SEO / social / business automation: **retainer up front, then billed hourly**. Invoice discipline.
- **Value framing leads**: what RDD saves/earns the client — cost per customer, lead conversion
  rate, "what is a converted lead worth". This is also the justification for a
  commission-on-lead option.
- **Scope definition first**, aligned to industry standards; explicit in-scope/out-of-scope split.

All ranges below are market ranges as reported by the cited sources — never invented precision.
All URLs accessed **2026-07-24**.

## b. Performance / commission-on-lead-conversion pricing

### Structures

1. **Per-qualified-lead (PPL):** client pays per delivered qualified lead. Reported US ranges:
   $20–$200/lead typical; home services $20–$80; legal/financial $200–$500+. High-ticket niches
   (a metal building project) sit at the top of, or above, these ranges.
2. **Revenue share:** agency takes a % of attributed revenue — typically 10–30% depending on margin
   and sales-cycle length. Works best with short cycles and clean attribution; breaks down with
   long, multi-touch B2B cycles like building projects.
3. **Hybrid base + performance (the industry-standard compromise):** reduced base retainer
   (~50–70% of standard) + performance bonus when metrics are exceeded. Reported examples:
   "$2,000/mo base + $50 per lead beyond the first 20" or "$3,000/mo + 10% of revenue above
   baseline."

### Pros

- Aligns agency incentive with client outcome; easy to sell against pure retainers.
- Low perceived risk for the client — "you only pay when it works."
- For RDD, directly monetizes the pinned value framing (what is a converted lead worth).

### Cons / risks (why pure commission is rarely standalone)

- **Qualified-lead definition disputes** — the #1 failure mode. "Qualified" must be specific,
  measurable, and agreed in writing before the campaign starts, or every invoice is an argument.
- **Attribution disputes** — multi-touch journeys (saw the truck, Googled the name, called) make
  "whose lead is this" contestable; performance models require an agreed attribution methodology
  and clean conversion tracking *before* any bonus accrues.
- **Volume-over-quality incentive** — paid-per-lead agencies optimize for count, not close-rate;
  some PPL vendors resell the same lead to multiple buyers.
- **Data-access friction** — revenue share requires visibility into the client's financials/close
  data, which most SMBs won't share; the agency must also trust the client's own reporting of
  which leads closed.
- **Cash-flow risk for the agency** — pure performance means unpaid work when the client's sales
  process (not the marketing) fails to close.

Industry sources consistently report that **pure performance pricing is rare in practice**; the
prevailing form is the hybrid (base retainer + performance kicker) — which matches the operator's
"add-on, not standalone" instinct exactly.

Citations:

- https://clicksgeek.com/lead-generation-agency-cost/ (accessed 2026-07-24) — PPL $20–$200/lead; legal/financial $200–$500+.
- https://ttmc.co.uk/knowledge/articles/pay-per-lead-a-penny-wise-pound-foolish-approach (accessed 2026-07-24) — PPL quality/volume incentive problems; lead resale.
- https://www.stackmatix.com/blog/performance-marketing-agency-pricing (accessed 2026-07-24) — hybrid base+bonus examples; rev-share 10–30%; attribution/data prerequisites; pure performance rare.
- https://www.dojoai.com/blog/the-agency-pricing-model-battle-fixed-fee-vs-percentage-vs-performance-based (accessed 2026-07-24) — fixed vs % vs success-fee trade-offs.
- https://sevenfigureagency.com/exploring-pay-per-performance-marketing-agency-pricing-models/ (accessed 2026-07-24) — pay-per-performance structuring for home-services/contractor agencies.

## c. Construction / metal-building niche

### c.1 Operator-pointed comp — Structure Webworks (structurewebworks.com)

Studied https://structurewebworks.com/ and https://structurewebworks.com/industries/contractors
(both accessed 2026-07-24). A contractors-niche web agency; directly comparable positioning for
"Ronin Building Design."

- **Positioning:** "SaaS, web apps & marketing *that turn clicks into customers*" — outcome-driven,
  accountability tied to client revenue metrics rather than billable hours.
- **Service packaging:** five services (Web Design · Web Applications · SEO & Core Web Vitals ·
  SaaS Platform · Digital Marketing). **No published price tiers.** Instead they sell process
  guarantees: *fixed quotes, no surprises* · *working demo every Friday* · *reply within 1 business
  day* · *brief to launch in 12 weeks*.
- **Contractors page structure:** headline value prop → platform overview → trade-specific tools →
  feature modules (Sales & Planning / Project Management / Financial Suite / Client Growth) →
  implementation process → FAQ. CTAs are "Book a call" / "Start a project."
- **Pricing framing:** "One fixed price you own" — explicitly contrasted with per-user SaaS
  subscriptions. No dollar amounts anywhere.
- **Takeaways for RDD:** (1) niche agencies in this space do NOT publish prices — they publish
  *process promises* and fix the price per-engagement, which validates the one-pager's
  "indicative, not a quote" stance; (2) the industries-page pattern (per-trade landing page with
  trade-specific vocabulary) is the model for a Ronin Building Design landing; (3) their
  fixed-price-with-demo-cadence framing is the market's answer to scope anxiety — RDD's
  change-order discipline is the same answer stated contractually.

### c.2 "PMBA" resolution — FLAG FOR OPERATOR CONFIRMATION

> **Conclusion: the operator most plausibly meant MBMA — the Metal Building Manufacturers
> Association (mbma.com).** A literal "PMBA" organization does not exist in the metal-building
> industry (searches for "PMBA metal building association" and "Pre-engineered Metal Building
> Association" return only MBMA). The likely path to the acronym: MBMA members' product is
> universally called a **PEMB** (pre-engineered metal building) — Mammoth's own PRD models the
> deal as "the PEMB Deal" — and "PEMB association" compresses naturally to "PMBA."
> **Confirm with Brian/Michael before citing it to the client.**

- **MBMA** (mbma.com): founded 1956, Cleveland OH; the trade association for metal building
  systems manufacturers; members produce ≈28,000 buildings/yr. Publishes exactly the kind of
  "guidelines + member sites" the operator described (see c.4).
- **Alternative checked — NFBA** (nfba.org): National Frame Building Association — the
  **post-frame (wood-frame/pole-barn)** industry, not metal building systems. Publishes technical
  resources, free code libraries, Frame Builder Magazine, and an accredited-builder program.
  Plausible only if Mammoth also sells post-frame; given "Mammoth **Metal** Buildings" and the
  PEMB deal model, MBMA is the better fit.
- **Literal "PMBA":** no hits as a building-industry association (nearest matches are unrelated:
  Philippine Metalworkers' Alliance, professional-MBA programs).

## d. Recommendation — the pricing-model matrix RDD presents to Michael

| Model | Structure | Indicative range | Market placement | When to use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **1. Fixed-Scope Build** | One-time firm-fixed price; scope locked in writing first; **any** change = written change order | **$8,000–$10,000** | Upper-mid SMB custom band; low-mid boutique-agency band — defensible, not premium-outlier | The site build itself; client wants cost certainty |
| **2. Time & Materials** | Hourly, invoiced monthly; estimates non-binding | **$100–200/hr** | Senior US web-dev band (senior avg ≈$128; $100–180+ routine); also solo-expert AI-consulting band | Evolving/undefined scope; post-launch feature work |
| **3. Retainer stack** (maintenance / SEO / social) | **Retainer paid up front, work billed hourly against it** (pinned model); unused hours per agreed rollover policy | Maintenance $100–300/mo · local SEO $500–1,500/mo · social $500–1,500/mo entry — each within cited market bands | Maintenance at market mid; SEO/social at the local/limited-competition tier where Mammoth competes | Ongoing growth services after launch |
| **4. AI-consulting / automation engagement** | Same $100–200/hr T&M, or fixed-scope per automation (each automation = its own tight scope + change control) | Hourly $100–200; small fixed projects **$2,500–$10,000** each (market: SMB automations $5K–$25K) | Solo-expert tier ($80–200/hr) — below boutique ($150–300), consistent rate card | Lead follow-up automation, CRM workflows, reporting |
| **5. Commission-on-lead (ADD-ON only)** | Hybrid rider on top of model 3: base retainer + per-**qualified**-lead bonus above an agreed monthly baseline; written qualified-lead definition + attribution method + cap | Bonus per qualified lead sized against the cited $20–200+/lead market and Mammoth's actual lead value (high-ticket → top of range) | The industry-standard hybrid form; pure performance is rare and disputed | Only after tracking exists and ≥1 quarter of baseline data; never standalone |

### Recommended default

**Fixed-Scope Build ($8–10K) + Retainer stack, with commission-on-lead offered as a later add-on
rider — model 1 + 3, quoting model 2 for anything outside a locked scope.**

Why:

1. **It matches both anchors at once.** The pinned constraints (firm-fixed only with change
   control; retainer-then-hourly for growth services) *are* the market's own best-practice answer
   — the Structure Webworks comp sells the identical shape ("one fixed price you own" + ongoing
   services, no published tiers).
2. **$8–10K is defensible without discounting.** It sits inside every cited SMB custom-build band
   and below boutique full-strategy pricing, while the 80-hour sanity math holds at the senior
   hourly benchmark.
3. **The value framing does the selling, not the price.** A converted metal-building lead is a
   five-to-six-figure project; against the cited $20–500+/qualified-lead market, even one
   incremental converted lead per quarter dominates the entire retainer cost. Lead with cost per
   customer and "what is a converted lead worth," then let the commission add-on be the proof of
   confidence — RDD only earns the kicker when Michael wins.
4. **Commission stays an add-on because the research says standalone fails**: qualified-lead
   definition disputes, attribution disputes, volume-over-quality incentives, and agency cash-flow
   risk are the documented failure modes; the hybrid base+bonus form is the one structure the
   industry reports working — and it requires tracking infrastructure (which the Mammoth CRM lane
   conveniently provides).

**Sequencing:** scope definition first (explicit in-scope/out-of-scope split, aligned to the
conventions in c.1/c.4) → Fixed-Scope Build → maintenance retainer at launch → SEO/social retainer
→ automation engagements as CRM matures → commission rider once attribution data exists.
